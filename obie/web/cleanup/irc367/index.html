Requirements of the project choice:

You should investigate a topic of interest to you in the area of parallel or distributed computing. You must implement some aspect of your project to show that you can manage the development of piece of software that is designed and built by you. Research that leads to a different or unusual way of implementing something in this area is very, very desirable, but it is acceptable to implement some algorithms that you have studied in research papers. Your programming must be substantial enough to demonstrate that you can implement a major programming project. You are expected to explore some journal articles about related work as well as research-oriented web sites. I expect you to locate most of your research sources, but I am willing to help whenever possible. Don't forget that the librarian, Jeff Wanser, Library 206, specializes in science research sources. He is very willing to help you locate material you may need.

The best projects have been those with the following characteristics:

1) A specific object, phenomena, or topic is selected to be investigated and is explored in depth.

2) Journal papers and research-oriented web sites that deal with parallel and distributed computing have been read and explored in order to investigate what others have done. OhioLink provides you with access to the ACM Digital Library Portal (see reference below) which contains many excellent articles. For example, searching on "simd" and "sorting" produced 200 articles! You can search it off campus, but you need a Hiram College machine to access the articles. Similarly, you can search at the IEEE Digital Library Portal (see reference below) and read abstracts, but you won't be able to download articles. Check with me as to whether or not I have any articles you are interested in.

3) The project is something the student has felt passionately and excited about. When this happens, the necessary time devoted to the project is not work, but fun. Although the IRC carries only 2 hours of credit, that amount was chosen to save you money, not to reflect on the amount of time you will work on the project.

4) The work proceeds evenly over the entire semester and isn't pushed just at the last moment. In this IRC, the learning curve was quite steep and you have 2/3 of semester to work on this. You need to move quickly to pick a topic, discuss your choice with me, and start your work.

Even with good progress all through the semester, you will tend to push at the end to add some things you just thought about doing. PLAN AHEAD TO ALLOW YOURSELF THE TIME!!!

Almost all failures or poor projects have been due to procrastination, a failure to realize the amount of time involved in doing a good job, or a failure to seek help in time.

    Your deliverables for this course will be:

    I. A formal proposal for your project.- Due as soon after your discussion with me as possible.

    This must be approximately a 3 page discussion of how you envision your project. Explain
    1) What you plan to implement- to the best of your knowledge right now.
    2) Why this project is of interest to you.
    3) Specify any web sites and journal articles you have explored already that are about your topic. Youshould include some citations to journal articles that might be of use to you.
    4) Describe how you plan to implement the project in stages.

    A staged approach is VERY critical as you will want to have a working project available throughout your entire development. Remember, start simple and add complexity to later versions. Always keep a WORKING version of your project safe (multiple backups are a good idea) and work on a copy to add enhancements. If you tell me at the presentation time that you had a working version last week and now it doesn't work, I will assume you have done nothing and your grade will be the same!

    Be sure to write your proposal (and your paper later) in good, scientific style --- i.e. no "I's".

    I am aware that you may be changing your ideas during the course of your development. That is fine, but if you don't have an initial vision of your project, you won't make any progress.

    mailto: obie@hiram.edu

    You may find you wish to adapt some existing code. It is possible to do this provided you:
    1) Document the source of every single line you use that is not yours in a way that I can find easily the original code and see it. Failure to do this will be a plagiarism violation.
    2) You have added a substantial amount of coding that can demonstrate your ability to code a large project. If in doubt, ask me before you assume you have done enough on your own. If a lot of your code is from another source, you have not met the requirements of this course.

    BE VERY CAREFUL ABOUT USING CODE FROM OTHER SOURCES. IT IS EASY TO SLIDE INTO A PLAGIARISM SITUATION. ASK IF YOU WANT ADVICE AS TO WHETHER OR NOT YOUR WORK IS ACCEPTABLE!


    II. Progress reports- weekly on Sunday after approval of your project. These should mention any journal articles you have read, comments about how your project is progressing, any successes you have had, and any problems you have had. Put in the title the words:

    Report (<date of the Sunday>)

    and mailto: obie@hiram.edu

    III.An abstract of your project - Due two days before your presentation

    IV. A paper written in a journal style- Due two days before your presentation

    • See samples and comments listed below.
    • Use scientific style -Please, no "I"s!

    V. A talk to be presented to an audience of peers, friends, and faculty during the last week of the 12 week period.

    Talks will be scheduled in the 12th week of the semester.

    • The talk should be 20 minutes in length with 10 minutes allowed for questioning.
    • Be sure to organize your talk so you can pack a lot of information AND a short demo into the time.
    • You should plan to stay after all of the talks so you can further demo your project to interested individuals.
    • Be sure to plan to be at all presentations as you are expected to participate in the evaluation and questioning of all of the presenters.

    VI. A poster suitable for a poster session at a conference. To be hung on the walls outside our offices in Colton or outside Colton 106.
    • We will use the huge line printer as we did in graphics. Details will be provided later.
    • You will need to design your poster using a huge PowerPoint slide.
    • The poster must be mounted outside C-106 and the CPSC offices 111-112 before the night of your talk.
    • Details on making the poster will be send by email shortly.

    VII. A final package of the project which you designed, developed, and coded- Due by the last day of the 12 week exam period.

    • Provide a CD which includes all of the above: Your final proposal, your abstract, your powerpoint slides for the talk, your paper, your source code (and instructions for building the executable image in a readme.txt file), an executable file of your code, and the powerpoint slide for your poster (this will be explained when we talk about posters). Label the CD with your name and CPSC 367.
